The Dialogue between “Om” and “Xiang”

Designed and executed a nine-month ethnographic fieldwork study, employing participant observation, in-depth interviews (n=100+), and visual/material analysis (700+ photographs), supplemented by statistical survey analysis (SPSS, NVivo). Proposed a "Nehulay-to-Stove Cat" transformation model, arguing that the local cat-worship phenomenon is a "carnivalesque resistance" that deconstructs the orthodox power discourse of Tibetan Buddhism. Demonstrated how stove art functions as a crucial medium for reshaping social memory and constructing local ethnic identity in the face of cultural modernization.

Embodied Knowledge and Cultural Transmission in Traditional Puppetry

Investigated the role of traditional puppetry in preserving intangible cultural heritage and constructing community identity. Conducted a three-month fieldwork, employing participant observation by training and performing as a member of a local troupe. Analyzed the transmission of embodied, non-textual artistic knowledge and the function of performance as a medium for social cohesion.

Ecologies of Heritage: Cultural Revitalization in Rural China

Conducted fieldwork on Bashao Island (蚆蛸岛), Dalian, Liaoning, specifically studying the customs and intangible cultural heritage of the islanders. Explored the connection between the community and the cultural ecosystem. Suggested a culturally sensitive framework for conserving and rejuvenating rural Chinese culture and folk traditions, showcasing the island's significance as a model.

From Ritual Governance to Aesthetic Transcendence: A Semiotic Genealogy of “乐”

Conducted a diachronic semiotic analysis of the polyphonic character “Lè/Yuè” (乐), mapping its trajectory across three dimensions. Investigated how the signifier evolved from ritual-political statecraft (Yuè) to secular embodied affect (Lè), and ultimately to cosmological transcendence. Drawing on historical aesthetics and textual analysis, argued this linguistic bifurcation indexes a transformation in Chinese subjectivity: moving from collective normative ordering toward a metaphysics of aesthetic liberation.

Motifs of Change

Led a fieldwork team investigating the folk craft and symbolic economy of animal imagery in Tibetan rug production. Combined a month of fieldwork with quantitative analysis of motif frequencies and artisan surveys to investigate the socio-political symbolism of animal imagery in Tibetan rugs, culminating in a 10,000+ word report.

Grassroots Governance and Community Dynamics in Urban China

Designed and executed a mixed-methods study on community governance across 7 ward-winning urban communities in Chengdu. Employed quantitative (136 valid questionnaires) and qualitative (50+ structured interviews) approaches to analyze the mechanisms of resident participation and service delivery. Developed a "One Core, Two Networks, Three Guarantees" governance framework, which was later adopted by three communities to guide their policy and practice. This research formed the basis of my B.A. thesis, which focused specifically on the role of resident participation.

Visual Culture and Material Memory in Chinese Revolutionary Heritage Sites

Participated in a two-year mixed-methods study on revolutionary heritage in the Sichuan-Shaanxi region, analyzing the visual and material strategies of public memorialization through comparative analysis, extensive interviews, and surveys across three cities (N=182, analyzed with SPSS). Co-authored a comprehensive research report with policy recommendations, which was officially adopted by three county-level cultural and tourism bureaus.
